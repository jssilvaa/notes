Physics-Informed Neural Networks — Forward and Inverse Problems {WIP}

Summary

PINNs encode PDE structure directly into a neural network’s loss by adding a collocation residual MSEf​ alongside the data-fit term MSEu​. Automatic differentiation (c.f. automatic-differentiation) through the network’s inputs provides the required spatial/temporal derivatives. From here, there are two problems we can deal with: in the forward problem, known physical parameters λ constrain the solution manifold; in the inverse problem, λ (contact stiffness, viscosity, actuator delay) is optimised together with the network weights - this is the practical use case for system identification on hardware where the URDF is wrong. Identifiability is governed by the rank of a Fisher information matrix F — a direct analogue of the rank-deficient outer Jacobian seen in active-set-sensitivity-differentiable-mpc.

Core Mechanism

General parametric PDE. Let u(t,x) be the latent solution and N a (non)linear differential operator to the problem:

ut​+N[u;λ]=0,x∈Ω,t∈[0,T]

We replace u(t,x) with a neural network nθ​(t,x) and define the residual network:

fθ​(t,x):=∂t​uθ​+N[uθ​;λ]

In the forward problem, we fit the NN to minimize the residual, with fixed physical parameters λ. In the inverse problem, we will want to minimize the residual, optimising both the network weights and the physical quantities we want to estimate.

All derivatives in fθ​ come from autodiff of uθ​ w.r.t. its inputs. fθ​ shares weights with uθ​ but has a different computational graph (what does this mean?). On a true PDE solution, fθ​≡0 (the residual is zero when the true solution is found).

Training loss (continuous time, forward).

L(θ)=MSEu​Nu​1​i∑​∣uθ​(tui​,xui​)−ui∣2​​+MSEf​Nf​1​j∑​∣fθ​(tfj​,xfj​)∣2​​

MSEu​ fits initial/boundary data; MSEf​ enforces the PDE at Nf​ collocation points. To fit the NN parameters to data, optimise with L-BFGS for Nu​≲103, or Adam for larger batches.

Discrete time variant. Apply an implicit Runge–Kutta scheme with q stages to avoid needing collocation points globally:

un+ci​=un−Δtj=1∑q​aij​N[un+cj​],i=1,…,q

Place a multi-output NN prior on {un+c1​,…,un+cq​,un+1}. The temporal truncation error is O(Δt2q); choosing q≈21​logϵ/log(Δt) drives it below machine precision, enabling very large time-steps (Δt=0.8 on Allen–Cahn with relative L2 error 6.99×10−3).

Inverse problem. Make Θphys​ (Raissi calls it λ, but it clashes with the loss weight λphys​) a learnable parameter:

L(θ,Θphys​)=Nu​1​i∑​∥uθ​(xi,ti)−ui⋆​∥2+λphys​Nf​1​j∑​∥N[uθ​;Θphys​](xj,tj)∥2+R(Θphys​)

R is a prior: ℓ2​ around URDF nominal, or 2α​∥Θphys​−ΘURDF​∥2 (MAP estimator - bounds identification error in terms of the data Fisher information).

Identifiability via Fisher information.

F=j∑​(∂Θ​Nj​)(∂Θ​Nj​)⊤

Θphys​ is identifiable ⇔ F has full rank near Θ⋆. Rank deficiency = the data does not excite enough modes to distinguish the parameters (persistence-of-excitation condition). The inverse problem becomes ill-posed not because the solver fails, but because the geometry of the data manifold collapses the relevant subspace.

SEA mapping. For a series-elastic actuator:

τreal​=Ks​(qm​−q)+Ds​(q˙​m​−q˙​),Jm​q¨​m​=τcmd​(t−δ)−τreal​

Θphys​=(Ks​,Ds​,Jm​,δ); uθ​ absorbs the residual Δτ on top of rigid-body inverse dynamics (friction nonlinearities). Ks​ vs Ds​ require distinct excitation: static hold (identifies Ks​) and chirp (identifies Ds​).

Why It Matters

In humanoid system identification, the URDF values for SEA stiffness, damping, and actuator delay are off by 10–30% from hardware. Standard RLS/UKF require a fixed parametric model for the residual dynamics; the PINN NN absorbs whatever the model misses while the small Θphys​ vector carries the interpretable physical values. Concretely: run a 10-minute chirp + push-recovery experiment on G1, stack diverse trajectories to achieve PE across all entries of F, and recover (Ks​,Ds​,Jm​,δ) jointly without writing a closed-form residual model.

Compute is tractable at modest scale: Raissi reports Burgers’ forward solve (Nu​=100, Nf​=10,000, 9-layer MLP, 20 neurons/layer) reaching relative L2 error 6.7×10−4 in 60s on a single Titan X — useful anchor for sizing experiments.

Secondary use case: the discrete-time model lets you take Δt=0.8s time-steps for a stiff PDE (Allen–Cahn, Burgers shock) that would require millions of explicit steps classically — effectively a high-order implicit integrator with free differentiation.

Limitations

  • Collocation scaling. Continuous-time models need Nf​ to grow exponentially in spatial dimension (curse of dimensionality). Sparse grid or QMC sampling only partially mitigates this; the discrete-time variant sidesteps it entirely at the cost of sequential stepping.
  • Loss conditioning. ∇θ​ and ∇Θphys​​ scale orders of magnitude differently. Fixed λphys​ leads to either ignoring data (too large) or leaving Θphys​ unidentified (too small). Wang & Perdikaris 2021 adaptive gradient-norm weighting is necessary in practice.
  • No convergence guarantee. L-BFGS has no global convergence certificate; the optimisation landscape is non-convex. Architectures that work for one PDE can fail silently for another (Krishnapriyan et al., NeurIPS 2021).
  • Hidden state. If the true system has unobserved state (e.g. joint temperature modulating friction), the inverse PINN converges to some average/envelope over that state — not the true Θphys​.
  • Second-derivative autodiff cost. N involving uxx​ requires differentiating through second derivatives of uθ​; graph memory grows with network depth. Separable PINNs / Hessian-free tricks required for deeper architectures.

Counterweight — robustness. Raissi’s KdV inverse experiment recovers λtrue​=(1,0.0025) as λrecovered​=(0.999,0.0024996) even at 1% noise on the data. The PDE residual term acts as an infinite-dimensional regulariser, and noise on the data does not propagate into Θphys​ as long as identifiability holds. This is a genuine advantage over RLS, which would absorb noise directly into the parameter estimate.

Open Questions

  • For the G1 SEA problem: what is the minimal experiment duration and signal content that makes F full-rank above the IMU noise floor? Can this be designed greedily (optimal experiment design)?
  • Is inverse PINN strictly better than UKF for SEA-ID, or is it UKF with a non-parametric residual model? The key difference: UKF requires the residual to be Gaussian; PINN’s NN absorbs arbitrary residuals — but at what sample cost?
  • Could a Hamiltonian/Lagrangian NN (Cranmer 2020) replace the URDF rigid-body model entirely, and what do you lose in extrapolation compared to retaining the URDF structure with PINN corrections?
  • Adaptive λphys​(t) from gradient-norm balancing (Wang 2021): is there a principled schedule rather than a heuristic, e.g. derived from the Fisher information ratio between data and residual terms?
  • Diagnostic carry-over from deck 02: monitor rank(F) during training as a real-time identifiability alarm. When rank drops, halt and request more excitation — instead of letting the loop converge to a manifold-projected Θphys​. Is this standard in the SINDy/PINN-ID literature?
