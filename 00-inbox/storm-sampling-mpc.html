STORM: An Integrated Framework for Fast Joint Space Model Predictive Control for Reactive Manipulation

STORM: An Integrated Framework for Fast Joint-Space Model-Predictive Control for Reactive Manipulation

May 26, 20265 min read

  • robotics/control

Summary

Task-space MPC for manipulators cannot enforce joint-space constraints (limits, singularities, link collisions); gradient-based joint-space MPC requires differentiable costs, which breaks on contact. STORM runs sampling-based MPC (MPPI variant) directly in joint space by fully tensorizing the kinematic rollout for GPU parallelism, replacing pseudorandom control sampling with Halton+B-spline sequences for smoothness, and plugging learned collision checkers in as non-differentiable cost terms. The result runs at 125 Hz (8ms) on a single GPU — 100× faster than prior MPPI manipulation implementations — and is validated on a Franka Panda doing dynamic tracking and ball balancing.

Core Mechanism

Policy and update. The policy is a factored Gaussian over open-loop control sequences: πϕt​​=∏h=0H−1​πϕt,h​​, parameterized by means μt​∈Rd×H and covariances Σt​∈Rd×H. The MPPI objective is the exponentiated utility:

L=Eπϕ​,P^​[exp(−β1​C^(xt​,ut​))],C^=h=0∑H−2​γhc^(x^t,h​,ut,h​)+γH−1q^​(x^t,H−1​)

Sample weights wi​=exp(−β1​C^i​), then update:

μt,h​=(1−αμ​)μt−1,h​+αμ​∑i​wi​∑i​wi​ut,h​​,Σt,h​=(1−ασ​)Σt−1,h​+ασ​∑i​wi​∑i​wi​(ut,h​−μt,h​)(ut,h​−μt,h​)⊤​

Diagonal per-joint covariance Σt,hU​=diag(σ1​,…,σd​) replaces the scalar σu​I of standard MPPI.

Kinematic rollout as a tensor operation. State x=[θ,θ˙,θ¨]∈R3d, control u=θ¨. Integrating N particles over horizon H in batch:

Θ¨=u,Θ˙=θ˙init​+Sl(1)​diag(dt)Θ¨,Θ=θinit​+Sl(1)​diag(dt)Θ˙

where Sl(1)​ is a lower-triangular ones matrix. The entire N×H batch is a single tensor product — no Python loop over the horizon. Variable dt (small near current state, large later) gives high-resolution cost near now and better cost-to-go approximation.

Sampling strategy. Pseudorandom samples cluster; Halton sequences use coprime-base van der Corput sequences to achieve near-uniform coverage of Rd. Controls are sampled once from Halton, then transformed by (μt​,Σt​). B-spline degree-3 fitting to the Halton samples, then sub-sampled at finer resolution, produces smooth joint acceleration trajectories — the comb filter alternative requires manual coefficient tuning and doesn’t guarantee smoothness between adjacent horizon steps.

Cost terms (non-exhaustive):

  • Stopping contingency: time-varying velocity limit θ˙max​=Su(1)​θ¨max​dt; penalizes states that can’t brake by horizon end.
  • Singularity avoidance: c^manip​=1−detJ(θ)J(θ)⊤​ when below threshold km​.
  • Self-collision: jointNERF — MLP trained to predict minimum inter-link distance from [sinθ,cosθ] (positional encoding improves accuracy); cost =max(0,jointNERF(θ)).
  • Environment collision: learned binary classifier from Danielczuk et al., operating on raw pointcloud vs. robot link pointcloud; outputs {0,1} — no SDF, no gradient required.

Why It Matters

This lets you run joint-space MPC with non-differentiable perception inputs (raw pointclouds, binary collision classifiers) at real-time rates. Previously the bottleneck was twofold: (a) task-space MPC required a separate low-level controller that couldn’t account for joint limits or link collisions, forcing manual constraint engineering; (b) joint-space gradient-based MPC (iLQR/DDP) broke the moment costs were discrete or contact was present. STORM’s concrete payoff: a Franka tracking a human-moved ball through a cluttered scene, where the robot spontaneously backs off when the elbow would clip an obstacle, then re-approaches — without any replanning trigger, purely from the continuous cost gradient.

The ball-balancing task is the stress test: simplified gravity-only ball dynamics, no friction, 30Hz noisy perception. MPC’s receding-horizon re-optimization absorbs the model bias that would make an open-loop plan diverge in under a second.

Limitations

  • Kinematics-only model: at higher speeds, unmodeled inertia and joint flexibility accumulate error; the model bias that MPC re-optimization tolerates in slow tasks will not cancel fast enough for, say, high-speed throws or impacts.
  • No state uncertainty propagation: particle rollouts start from a point estimate xt​; state estimation noise is not propagated through the horizon. Under high observation noise (fast moving target + slow camera), the controller is effectively open-loop between perception updates.
  • Halton sequences are deterministic: the same N Halton points are used every cycle (transformed by current μ,Σ). If the optimal control lies outside the current distribution’s support, the fixed Halton grid provides no escape — the algorithm can stall in local minima not recoverable by covariance adaptation alone.
  • Learned collision costs require offline training per robot: jointNERF must be retrained for each robot morphology; the environment pointcloud classifier also requires a training distribution that may not transfer to novel objects.
  • No hard constraint satisfaction: all constraints (joint limits, collision) enter as soft cost terms. High-weight penalties approximate constraint satisfaction but don’t guarantee it, particularly for sudden obstacles at close range within a single horizon.

Open Questions

  • Can a learned terminal Q-function (as suggested by the authors) replace the kinematic terminal cost and absorb model-bias due to dynamics, without losing the 8ms budget?
  • What is the right way to propagate state estimation covariance through the kinematic rollout — sigma-point / unscented transform seems tractable given the tensor structure, but what does it cost in wall-clock time?
  • Halton sequences fix coverage, but the distribution they’re drawn from (μt​,Σt​) is still unimodal Gaussian — how badly does this fail on multimodal cost landscapes (e.g., two equally valid paths around an obstacle)?
  • The B-spline smoothing operates on joint accelerations but the cost is evaluated on joint positions — is there a formulation that directly samples smooth position trajectories (e.g., via trajectory optimization with smoothness priors) that would reduce the approximation gap?
